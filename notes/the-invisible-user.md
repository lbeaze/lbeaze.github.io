---
title: The Invisible User
date: 2026-03-22
tags: accessibility, inclusion, design
---

The users you hear from are not representative of all your users. They're the ones who had the time and ability to file a bug report, write an email, or find the feedback form. The people who bounced because navigation didn't make sense, who couldn't complete a purchase because the modal trapped their focus, who gave up because the error message told them nothing — those users don't appear in your qualitative data. They just leave.

This is why accessibility work is fundamentally different from feature feedback cycles. You can't wait for complaints. The people most affected by poor accessibility are often the least positioned to advocate loudly for themselves in your feedback channels.

Designing accessibly means designing for users you will never hear from. It requires building that user into your mental model from the start — not as an edge case to accommodate at the end, but as part of the primary audience your design has to work for.
